Missions

Europe's flagship Earth observation and meteorological missions — what they do, why they matter, and where I fit in.

01
CO2M / CRISTAL / LSTM / ROSE-L — ESA Copernicus Expansion Missions
Four of ESA's next-generation Copernicus satellites, each targeting a distinct dimension of Earth system monitoring. CO2M (Copernicus Anthropogenic CO₂ Monitoring) will be the first mission to measure anthropogenic carbon dioxide emissions from space at the source level — providing independent data to support the Paris Agreement. CRISTAL (Copernicus Polar Ice and Snow Topography Altimeter) carries a dual-frequency radar altimeter to monitor Arctic and Antarctic ice sheets, sea-ice thickness, and glaciers. LSTM (Land Surface Temperature Monitoring) will deliver high-resolution thermal data for agricultural monitoring, urban heat mapping, and water stress detection. ROSE-L (Radar Observation System for Europe in L-band) is a synthetic aperture radar mission providing all-weather, day-and-night surface deformation and biomass monitoring.

02
EPS-SG — EUMETSAT Polar System – Second Generation
EPS-SG is the successor to EUMETSAT's highly successful Metop series, designed to provide continuity of polar-orbiting meteorological observations through the 2040s. The Metop-SG satellites carry an expanded instrument suite — including Sentinel-5 for atmospheric chemistry, MWS for microwave temperature and humidity sounding, and IASI-NG for infrared atmospheric sounding — making it the most capable polar meteorological system ever built. Data from EPS-SG feeds directly into numerical weather prediction models used by forecasting centres worldwide, with major improvements in forecast accuracy expected over its predecessor.

03
EPS — EUMETSAT Polar System (Metop Fleet)
The original EPS, built around the Metop series of satellites (A, B, C), established Europe's polar-orbiting meteorological capability and became a cornerstone of global weather forecasting for over two decades. Operating in a sun-synchronous orbit at 817 km, the Metop satellites carry instruments measuring atmospheric temperature and humidity profiles, ozone, sea-surface winds, and soil moisture. The mission also supported co-operative operations with NOAA (NOAA-18/19), CNES (Jason-2/3, Saral), and the EARS ground network — making it a genuinely international operational programme.
